A Sonnet to a Rainbow

by The One Team

You are that band of light that splits the sky

The bridge that glistens far above the earth

And if we keep a watchful weather eye

We'll see, through clouds, your promise of new birth

For when storms rage and light is fading fast

And hope seems to have turned its back and fled

We know that bad times surely cannot last.

Then, as your colours bloom and shine and spread,

Slipping through clouds, your light is felt by all.

You speak no words and do not have a voice

Yet in their heart each person hears your call.

They rush outside to see you and rejoice.

Rainbow, you paint our skies so beautifully

Refracting joy for all the world to see.

D o w n !

There may be a storm on the horizon...

...but eventually the clouds will part and the sun will shine...

History of Rainbows

Rainbows have been a source of intrigue and debate for thousands of years. The ancient Greeks originally thought that they were only comprised of three colours: purple, yellow and red. It wasn't until after Rene Descartes discovered that they are caused by light from the sun being split into different colours by diffraction through raindrops in 1637 that Newton decided on the seven colours that we typically agree on today. Interestingly, China still uses the five colours that were agreed on in the 17th century: red, yellow, green, purple and blue.

Mythology of Rainbows

Many cultures view the rainbow as a bridge. Both Ancient Norse and Navajo traditions believed that they were bridges between the human world and the gods. Some ancient Japanese cultures saw the rainbow as a bridge that their ancestors would use to descend back to the earth and there is a Maori legend that it is the bridge that the moon created to allow her mortal human husband to return to earth so that he could rest with his own kind.

Rainbows are so culturally important that they even feature in the earliest written evidence of human civilisation from around 3000BC!

Notable Others:
  • In Native American traditions, the rainbow is thought to be the "hem of the sun's coat".
  • The Fang of Gabon in Africa are initiated into their religion by a "transcendent experience when they arrive at the rainbow's centre"
  • In Christianity, the rainbow is a symbol of God's promise to humanity - "I have set my rainbow in the sky and it will be the sign of the covenant between me and the Earth" (Geneis 9:13)
  • The "Whirling Rainbow" is a Native American prophecy that foretells of a day when "people of all races... will put aside their differences. They will come together in love, joinig hands in unification, to heal the Earth and all Her Children"

Science of Rainbows

Rainbows are a weather phenomenon whereby a circular or semi-circular spectrum of light appears in the sky. This is caused by the reflection, refraction and dispersion of light within rain droplets.

When light meets a raindrop, some of it is reflected and some enters the rainbow. The fraction which enters the rainbows is refracted (the direction of a wave is changed due to different propagation speeds in different media). Light of different colours is refracted at a different angles, and so takes slightly different paths. Once the light has passed through the droplet, some is reflected back into the droplet, a phenomenon known as total internal reflection. This reflected light then passes back through the rainbow, this can again be reflected back into the droplet, but the fraction that exits forms the primary rainbow (secondary, fainter rainbows are caused by multiple internal reflections). The refraction of the light within the rainbow has caused it to split into the spectrum, with the light having been reflected by approximately 42 degrees. The circular shape of the rainbow is caused by this 42 degree angle; the observer will see a curved arc of light reflected back through rain droplets at 42 degrees from the sun. Secondary rainbows can appear due to multiple internal reflections in the rain droplets, causing a greater angle of reflection (and hence these appear separately in the sky).
